CAN BCIs BE DESIGNED TO RESTORE/ENHANCE FUNCTIONS IN DAMAGED/DISEASED BRAIN TISSUE?

Yes, BCI’s can restore function in brain tissue afflicted by damage or disease. In fact BCIs have huge potential for neurorehabilitation. They could help people with motor disabilities due to stroke or spinal cord injuries by translating brain signals to motor commands, and transmitting such signals to prosthetics. This could give afflicted individuals independence and function that would otherwise be lost ​( SpringerOpen )​​ ( Frontiers )​​.

For example, BCIs can facilitate motor rehabilitation through mental imagery. Mental imagery is thought to keep neural circuits involved, promoting neuroplasticity–the brain’s ability to form new neural connections.​ ( Frontiers )​. This neuroplasticity could also aid in the natural healing process of damaged brain tissue, for instance tissue damaged by blunt force, such as a concussion. This could potentially lead to significant improvements in quality of life of individuals with brain-injuries​ ( MDPI )​.

Simply put, there are significant challenges in the way of using BCIs to restore the brain tissue. However scientific innovation in this field to harness the potential of BCIs could be beyond revolutionary in aiding individuals with impaired brain function.
